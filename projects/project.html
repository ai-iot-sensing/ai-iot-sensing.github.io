Introduction

We present a large-scale WiFi sensing dataset and benchmark designed to reflect the challenges of real-world deployments. Our dataset includes over X hours of data collected with diverse IoT devices across multiple environments and users. Covering a suite of practical sensing tasks—including activity recognition, localization, proximity detection, motion source recognition, breathing estimation and presence detection—this benchmark supports evaluation under in-distribution, cross-domain, and few-shot settings. We provide strong baselines including standard supervised models, a robust environment-invariant method, and meta-learning approaches. Our benchmark reveals significant generalization gaps and serves as a platform for advancing practical machine learning for wireless sensing.

Data collection

Although users could move about naturally and perform tasks as they would in daily life, we established basic data-collection protocols to ensure repeatability. At the start of each session, participants completed a brief calibration phase to check device connectivity, verify timestamps, and confirm stable CSI logging. Tasks ranged from remaining still in a seated position to walking between rooms, opening doors, or carrying objects through hallways, so that a variety of motion patterns and occlusions would be captured. Whenever possible, time-aligned external information was collected through camera recordings or local sensor logs to annotate user activities or highlight environmental changes. We also used cross-device and cross-packet synchronization methods—either relying on a common Network Time Protocol server or exchanging time-stamped handshake packets—to mitigate clock drift among multiple CSI-capturing devices. To track the distinct tasks, user states, or interference patterns, participants occasionally provided notes on events such as large file downloads, video streaming, or additional network congestion in the area. Although not strictly scripted, this approach yielded structured data across heterogeneous real-world scenarios with consistent referencing to ground-truth events.
